Website

A short guide to briefing a website designer

The smoother a website project goes, the clearer the brief was at the start.

A lot of website projects slow down for the same reason: the brief was too vague to start working from. Not because anyone did anything wrong, but because "build us a modern website" doesn't give a designer much to design around. A bit of preparation before the first conversation can save weeks later on.

Start with the goal, not the design

Before talking about colours or layout, it helps to be clear on what the site actually needs to do. Is it mainly there to explain what you do and build trust? Generate enquiries? Sell products directly? Support an existing sales process? The answer changes what the homepage prioritises, what the calls to action say, and how many steps it takes to get from "landed on the site" to "did the thing we wanted".

Show, don't just tell

Words like "clean", "modern" or "professional" mean different things to different people. A handful of example websites — both ones you like and ones you don't — give a designer something concrete to work from. It doesn't matter if they're in your industry or not; what matters is being able to point at something and say "something like this" or "definitely not like this".

Get your content sorted early

Design and content are easier to do together than one after the other. Having a rough idea of what each page needs to say — even as bullet points or a voice note — means the layout can be built around real content instead of placeholder text that gets reshuffled later. The same goes for logos, photos and any brand guidelines that already exist; gathering these upfront avoids a lot of back-and-forth mid-project.

None of this needs to be perfect or formal. A short document or even a focused conversation covering the goal, a few reference sites, and what content is ready (or not) is usually enough to get a project started on solid ground — and keep it there.
